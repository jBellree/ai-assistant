# Tutoring Notes: Personal Tax Returns

---

## Chunk 1: What is a personal tax return?

- A tax return is a form submitted to HMRC declaring all income received in a tax year.
- The official name is the **SA100** (also called a Self Assessment return).
- Not everyone files one — only people with income outside PAYE: self-employed, directors receiving dividends, high earners, landlords, etc.
- The UK tax year runs **6 April to 5 April** (not calendar year).
- Filing deadline: 31 January online for the previous tax year.

**Why this matters for DSG:**
Ltd co directors typically take a small PAYE salary and top up via dividends. The PAYE salary shows up automatically with employers. The dividends don't — they only appear on the tax return. So for these customers, the tax return is the only window into their real personal income picture.

## Chunk 2: The documents you'll actually see

There are four main documents. For HNW and Ltd co customers, DSG gets and needs the full SA100 — not just summaries.

### SA100 — the full tax return

- The master document. Covers all income sources: salary, dividends, rental, self-employment profit, etc.
- For commercial lenders (Aldermore, UTB, Haydock, Novuna, JBR, Arkle, Metro, Cambridge and Counties etc.) financing HNW customers and Ltd companies, the full SA100 is typically required.
- These customers have a "story" — the detail lives in the full document, not just the totals.
- DSG gets the full document and needs to be able to read it.

### SA302 — the tax calculation summary

- A summary generated by HMRC after submission.
- Shows: total income from all sources, tax owed, tax paid.
- Produced by HMRC (not the customer) so harder to manipulate — lenders trust it.
- Useful as a cross-reference but not sufficient on its own for complex customers.

### Tax Year Overview

- Separate HMRC document confirming whether the tax bill was actually paid.
- SA302 = what was owed. Tax Year Overview = whether it was paid.
- An unpaid tax bill is a hidden liability. If a customer owes HMRC £40k and hasn't paid it, that changes the picture.

### Supplementary pages

- Extra sheets attached to the SA100 for specific income types:
  - **SA103** — self-employment income (sole traders)
  - **SA102** — employment income
  - **SA105** — rental income
  - **SA101** — additional income (dividends, savings interest)
- SA302 gives the totals. Supplementary pages show the breakdown.
- Not always provided — but if a customer has multiple income streams, this is where the detail lives.

## Chunk 3: How a Ltd co director's income actually works — and why the SA100 can be misleading

### The basic structure

A Ltd co director typically manages their income in three ways:

1. **Small PAYE salary** — deliberately kept low (often around the personal allowance, ~£12,500, or the NI threshold) to minimise income tax and National Insurance. This shows up on the SA100 as employment income.
2. **Dividends** — profit drawn from the company. Taxed at a lower rate than salary. Shows up on the SA100 under dividends (SA101 supplementary page).
3. **Director's loan account (DLA)** — see below.

**The trap:** When an SA100 shows income of £12,500 and the customer wants to borrow £150k, it looks impossible. But if that £12,500 is just the PAYE salary and there are £80k of dividends on top — or a healthy DLA — the picture is completely different. The team's job is to find the full picture and explain it.

### The Director's Loan Account (DLA)

A DLA is a running account between the director and their company. It records money flowing in both directions.

**Two directions:**

| Direction | What it means | Flag for DSG |
|---|---|---|
| Director owes company (overdrawn DLA) | Director has taken more out than put in. Company is lending them money. Must be repaid within 9 months of company year end or company faces a tax penalty. | Potential red flag — it's a liability. Understand the size and repayment plan. |
| Company owes director (credit DLA) | Director has put more in than taken out — e.g. lent the company money early on. | Green flag — director has skin in the game. |

**Income credited to the DLA instead of drawn:**
A director can leave profit in the company and credit it to their DLA rather than drawing it as salary or dividend. No personal tax is triggered at that point. They can draw it down later in chunks. This money won't appear on the SA100 at all — because it hasn't been "received" in HMRC's eyes yet.

**The implication:** A customer can have an SA100 that looks thin but a DLA credit balance of £200k sitting ready to draw. That's a completely different risk profile.

### Why the SA100 alone isn't enough for a Ltd co director

The DLA balance lives in the **company accounts** (on the balance sheet, under liabilities — because the company owes it to the director). The SA100 won't show it.

The full picture for a Ltd co director usually needs:

- **SA100** — what they've drawn and declared personally
- **Company accounts** — to see the DLA balance and company profit
- **Sometimes:** a letter from the accountant explaining the income structure

### The core skill

The team isn't just reading numbers. They're building a narrative:

- What does the SA100 show?
- What's missing from it, and why?
- What do the company accounts add to the picture?
- How do we explain this structure to an underwriter in a way that gets the deal approved?

This is "painting a picture" — taking a set of documents that look confusing in isolation and presenting them as a coherent, honest story.

## Chunk 4: What the SA100 actually looks like — section by section

The SA100 is a multi-page form. Looks intimidating but laid out logically.

### The front page (pages 1-2)

Basic identification only. Name, address, UTR (Unique Taxpayer Reference, HMRC's ID for the customer), National Insurance number, and the tax year covered. Always check the tax year first.

### Section by section — where the money is

| Section | What it covers | Relevant for DSG when... |
|---|---|---|
| Employment | PAYE salary from one or more employers | Always — even directors will have a small salary here |
| Self-employment | Profit from sole trader work | Sole trader customers |
| UK property | Rental income | Customer is a landlord — adds to income picture |
| Dividends | Dividends from UK companies | Ltd co directors — often the big number |
| Interest | Savings interest | Minor for most, but shows wealth |
| Other income | Pension, foreign income, etc. | Catch-all |
| Tax reliefs | Gift Aid, pension contributions, etc. | Reduces taxable income |

For a typical Ltd co director: go straight to **Employment** (small PAYE salary) and **Dividends** (real income).

### Tax calculation at the end

Total income, total tax owed. SA302 reproduces this in cleaner format. If you have both, they should match.

### Supplementary pages

Separate sheets attached to the SA100 (SA101, SA102, SA103 etc — see Chunk 2). Sole trader: SA103 has turnover, expenses, profit. Director with dividends: SA101 has the dividend breakdown.

### Practical approach — what to do when an SA100 lands

1. Check front page — which tax year? Most recent completed?
2. Employment section — what's the PAYE salary?
3. Dividends section — what was drawn?
4. Check any other income sections (rental, self-employment, etc.)
5. Total income figure at the end.
6. Cross-reference with SA302 if available — totals should match.
7. Note absences. If no dividends but it's a Ltd co director, ask why.

### What lenders ask for

The latest tax return only. For that year, DSG provides both documents together:

- **SA100** — the full tax return (what was submitted)
- **SA302** — HMRC's tax calculation summary for that return

Two documents, same year. They should match each other. If they don't, something's wrong and worth querying before the lender spots it.
