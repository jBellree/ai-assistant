## Chunk 5: Company accounts — the other half of the picture

### What "company accounts" means

Every UK limited company files accounts each year in two places:

- **Companies House** — public record. Anyone can look these up free at companieshouse.gov.uk.
- **HMRC** — for tax purposes. More detailed. Not public.

For DSG, the version that matters is the Companies House version — that's what customers and accountants send over, and what lenders work from. Arrives as a PDF. Can come from either the customer or their accountant — documents are the same either way.

A company's financial year does not have to match the UK tax year. It can run any 12-month period (April to March, January to December, etc.). Always check the dates on the accounts.

### The two formats you'll see

**Micro-entity accounts**

Smallest companies only (turnover under £632k, balance sheet under £316k, fewer than 10 employees). Almost no useful information — just a basic balance sheet. No profit and loss account in the public filing.

When you see micro-entity accounts: ask for the full accounts filed with HMRC, which includes the profit and loss. Without it you can't see what the company actually earned.

**Small company accounts**

Slightly larger companies. These include:

- **Profit and loss account (P&L)** — what the company earned and spent over the year
- **Balance sheet** — snapshot of what the company owns and owes on one specific date
- **Notes to the accounts** — explanations behind the numbers. This is where the Director's Loan Account balance lives.
- **Director's report** — brief summary, rarely useful for DSG

Want the version with the P&L. If it's missing, ask for it.

### The three things DSG is actually looking for

1. **Profit and loss account** — how much did the company make?
2. **Balance sheet** — how financially strong is the company?
3. **Notes to the accounts** — specifically the Director's Loan Account (DLA) balance

### Practical first move when accounts land

1. Check company name matches the customer.
2. Check the financial year end date — how recent? More than 18 months old is stale.
3. Check the format — micro-entity? Ask for full accounts. Small company with P&L? You can work with it.
4. Note the Companies House number — useful for cross-referencing at companieshouse.gov.uk if needed.

## Chunk 6: Reading the profit and loss account (P&L)

The P&L answers one question: did the company make money this year, and how much? It covers a period of time (the financial year), not a single date.

### The structure — top to bottom

```
Turnover (also called Revenue or Sales)
minus  Cost of sales
equals Gross profit

minus  Overheads (also called Administrative expenses)
equals Operating profit

minus  Interest / finance charges
equals Profit before tax

minus  Corporation tax
equals Profit after tax (also called Net profit)
```

### What each line means

**Turnover** — total money the company brought in before any costs. Top line. For a consultancy director, this is total fees billed for the year.

**Cost of sales** — direct costs of delivering that turnover. Minimal for a consultancy. Larger for a construction company (materials, subcontractors). Not all companies have significant cost of sales.

**Gross profit** — turnover minus cost of sales. What's left before general running costs.

**Overheads / administrative expenses** — general running costs: rent, salaries (including the director's PAYE salary), software, insurance, professional fees. The director's salary sits here as a cost to the company.

**Operating profit** — gross profit minus overheads. What the business earned from its operations before financing and tax. The core health number.

**Profit before tax** — operating profit after interest charges (e.g. on a business loan). Close to operating profit for most small companies.

**Profit after tax** — what's left after corporation tax. This is the profit available to pay as dividends — or to leave in the company.

### What DSG is actually looking for

1. **Is the company profitable?** Negative profit after tax is a red flag.
2. **Does profit support the dividends taken?** If the director took £80k in dividends but profit after tax was only £30k, the extra £50k came from retained earnings (see below). Understand why.
3. **Does turnover match the customer's story?** If someone claims £200k income but company turnover is £150k, the numbers don't add up.

### Retained earnings — what they are and why they matter

Every year, profit after tax can either be paid out as a dividend or left in the company. Profit left in the company accumulates over time — this is called **retained earnings**. Think of it as the company's savings account built up from previous years' profits.

A director can draw dividends from retained earnings at any time, not just from this year's profit. So dividends can legally exceed this year's profit if there's a sufficient pot of retained earnings.

Why it matters: if retained earnings are large and the company is healthy, drawing more than this year's profit is fine. If the pot is nearly empty and profits are thin, the director may not be able to sustain that dividend level going forward.

The retained earnings figure lives on the **balance sheet** — covered in Chunk 7.

## Chunk 7: The balance sheet

The balance sheet answers a different question to the P&L. The P&L asks "did the company make money this year?" The balance sheet asks "what is the financial position of the company right now?" It's a snapshot on one specific date — the last day of the company's financial year.

### The basic structure

```
What the company OWNS (assets)
minus  What the company OWES (liabilities)
equals What belongs to the owners (equity)

Assets - Liabilities = Equity
```

Both sides must always equal each other. That's why it's called a balance sheet.

### Assets — what the company owns

**Fixed assets (non-current assets)**
Things owned long-term to run the business. Equipment, vehicles, property, computers.

**Current assets**
Short-term items that will turn into cash soon:
- Cash in the bank
- Money owed to the company by customers (debtors / trade receivables)
- Stock (if relevant)

### Liabilities — what the company owes

**Current liabilities** (due within 12 months)
- Money owed to suppliers (creditors)
- VAT and PAYE owed to HMRC
- Short-term loans
- Overdrawn DLA (director owes the company — appears here)

**Long-term liabilities** (due after 12 months)
- Business loans, property mortgages, etc.

### Equity — what belongs to the owners

**Share capital**
Original money put in when the company was set up. Usually tiny — often £1 or £100 for a small Ltd co.

**Retained earnings**
All profit the company has ever made, minus all dividends ever paid out. Grows every profitable year, shrinks when dividends are paid. Large positive retained earnings = company has been building resources over time. This is the pot a director draws dividends from when they exceed this year's profit.

### Where the DLA appears on the balance sheet

Trips people up — it can appear in two different places:

| DLA direction | What it means | Where it appears |
|---|---|---|
| Company owes the director (credit DLA) | Director put more in than taken out | Current liabilities |
| Director owes the company (overdrawn DLA) | Director took more out than put in | Current assets |

Always check the notes to the accounts — there will be a specific DLA note showing the balance and sometimes movement during the year.

### What DSG is looking for on the balance sheet

1. **Is equity positive?** If liabilities exceed assets, equity is negative — the company owes more than it owns. Serious red flag.
2. **Are retained earnings healthy?** Large positive figure = company has been profitable over time and hasn't stripped all cash out. Supports the director's ability to sustain dividend income.
3. **What's the DLA balance and which direction?** Credit DLA (company owes director) = fine, potentially a green flag. Overdrawn DLA (director owes company) = understand the size and repayment plan.
