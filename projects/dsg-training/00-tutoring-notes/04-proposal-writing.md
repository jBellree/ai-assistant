# Tutoring Notes: Writing the Proposal

---

## Chunk 9: Writing the proposal the DSG way

Everything covered in Chunks 1-8 is input. The proposal is the output. This is where reading documents turns into getting deals approved.

### The golden rule

Underwriters scan, they don't read. You have about 30 seconds to make your case before they form an opinion. Get to the point, lead with the strength, make it easy to say yes.

### The structure — every proposal, every time

```
1. The headline (one line)
2. The customer (3-5 bullets)
3. The deal (3-5 bullets)
4. Any weaknesses (1-2 bullets, brief and honest)
5. The ask (one line)
```

Five sections. One page. Every time.

### 1. The headline

One sentence. The single strongest thing about this application. Write it first.

Examples:
- "Sole director of a profitable IT consultancy, £380k turnover, conservative on personal drawings."
- "City solicitor, employed 12 years, £195k gross salary, clean credit."
- "Established sole trader, 15 years trading, consistent self-employment income of £85k."

If you can't write the headline, you don't understand the application yet. Go back to the documents.

### 2. The customer

Facts that build the picture of who this person is and why they're a good risk. Pull from the SA100 and company accounts.

For a Ltd co director:
- Director and 100% shareholder of [Company Name] Ltd, incorporated [year], [sector]
- Company turnover £[x], profit after tax £[x] (year ending [date])
- Personal income: £[x] PAYE salary plus £[x] dividends — total £[x]
- Retained earnings £[x] — director has been conservative on drawings
- DLA: credit/overdrawn balance £[x] — [brief explanation]

### 3. The deal

- Asset: [make, model, year, condition]
- Cash price: £[x]
- Deposit: £[x] ([x]%)
- Amount financed: £[x]
- Term: [x] months
- Monthly payment: £[x]
- Balloon (if applicable): £[x]

### 4. Weaknesses — brief and honest

Don't hide weaknesses. The underwriter will find them. If you've buried something they'll trust you less.

One or two bullets, factual, with context:
- "Declared personal income of £50k reflects a deliberate dividend strategy, not the ceiling of what the company can support."
- "Company incorporated 2021 — three years trading, accounts available for two full years."
- "One missed payment on credit file, [date], now satisfied."

Address it, explain it briefly, move on.

### 5. The ask

One line:
- "Requesting approval at the terms above."
- "Would welcome approval subject to sight of latest company accounts."

### What never to write

- Adjectives that don't do real work: "strong," "robust," "established," "impressive"
- Paragraphs — if longer than two lines, break into bullets
- Repetition — say it once
- Anything that sounds like a template — underwriters spot it instantly

### Full example — James, McLaren 720S, £144k financed

---

*Sole director of a profitable IT consultancy, £380k turnover, consistently profitable, conservative on personal drawings.*

**Customer**
- Director and 100% shareholder of James Consulting Ltd, incorporated 2018, IT sector
- Company turnover £380k, profit after tax £95k (year ending March 2024)
- Personal income: £12,500 PAYE plus £37,500 dividends, total £50k
- Retained earnings £210k — well-capitalised, director has not stripped the business
- DLA: credit balance £45k — director has personally supported the company

**The deal**
- Asset: 2023 McLaren 720S, 8,000 miles
- Cash price: £180,000
- Deposit: £36,000 (20%)
- Amount financed: £144,000
- Term: 48 months
- Balloon: £72,000

**Note**
- Declared personal income of £50k reflects a deliberate low-salary, dividend-based structure — not a ceiling. Company profit after tax of £95k could support significantly higher drawings.

*Requesting approval at the terms above.*
